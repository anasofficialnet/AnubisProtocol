RECOVERED FROM CHAMBER KH-III

𓁟 The Naming of the Guardian

Among the scribes of the royal court, naming conventions followed strict hierarchical rules passed down through generations. Each scribe was assigned a ceremonial title composed of a personal identifier and the markers of office that reflected their station within the temple hierarchy.

The excavation team recovered a cartouche fragment from the antechamber of King Unas that references the guardian of this tomb. According to Professor Henriksen's translation notes, the personal identifier translates phonetically from the hieratic script. The glyphs spell a name that was common among the priestly class of Upper Egypt - a name that carried the weight of the desert wind: khasem. This was the name whispered in the corridors of the Temple of Thoth, spoken only by those who had earned the right to address the keeper of the northern archive.

The remaining markers of his station were recorded separately, as was customary to prevent any single record from revealing a guardian's complete identity. The archaeological team believes those fragments still survive somewhere within the tomb's scattered records.

𓂝 On the Nature of Ancient Identifiers

It is worth noting that in the ancient naming system, the personal name and each marker of office were joined by the sacred bridge-mark - the low connecting line later rendered as an underscore. The bridge was repeated between each division of station so that the living and the ceremonial could be spoken as one.

Many scholars have attempted to reconstruct full identifiers from partial cartouches, but without the remaining fragments, the name remains incomplete. The priests were meticulous in their fragmentation - no single chamber was ever allowed to hold the complete key to a guardian's identity.

The survey team catalogued every response from the excavation server under reference codes. Dr. Henriksen noted that the first sacred word was always filed alongside the site's primary designation. Those who inspect the server's response carefully may find traces of the old catalogue system still embedded in the protocol headers.
